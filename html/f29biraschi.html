Translation

Strab. 13, 1, 43: It is believed that Mount Ida is pointedly called "many-fountained" because of the great number of rivers taking their spring from there, in particular in the region where the Dardanian plain lies beneath it until Scepsis and the territory of Ilion.

[T5] Demetrios, being, as a native, well-acquainted with these places, spoke once in this way about them:
(Demetrios:) "there is indeed a hill of Mount Ida, called Kotylos and it lies about a hundred and twenty stadia above Scepsis; from it flow the Scamander, the Granicos and the Aesepus",

the two latter, towards the north and the Propontis, taking their sources from many springs, whereas the former does it from one source towards the south. All springs are, however, located close to each other in a radius of twenty stadia. The mouth of the Aesepus is located the farthest away from its source, at a distance of about five hundred stadia. There is a debate about what the poet meant by:

Il. 22. 147-52: "they reached the two fair-flowing fountains; there the two springs of the eddying Scamander have their sources. The one flows with tepid water ..."...

(which means warm). He then pursues:

..."...and all around smoke arises from it, as if from a fire. The other flows cold, as hail or wintery snow."

Indeed neither can warm water be found in the place now, nor is the spring of the Scamander there; it is instead in the mountain. Further there is one, not two of them. It is thus likely that the warm spring dried up and that the cold one, flowing from the Scamander through a subterranean channel, reappears at this place, or that, because it is close to the Scamander, also this water is called the spring of the Scamander (for, many sources are, in this way, attributed to a same river).

Strab. 13, 1, 44: And into the Scamander streams the Andiros, coming from the Karesene, a hilly region scattered with many villages and beautifully cultivated, which is adjacent to the Dardanian plain until the territories of Zeleia and Pityeia. It is said that the region takes its name from the river Karesos which is mentioned by name by the poet:

Il. 12. 20: "the Rhesos, the Heptaporos, the Karesos and the Rhodios";

but that the city which had the same name as the river was destroyed.
