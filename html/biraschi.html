Translation

Strab. 13, 1, 43: It is believed that Mount Ida is pointedly called "many-fountained" because of the great number of rivers taking their spring from there, in particular in the region where the Dardanian plain lies beneath it until Scepsis and the territory of Ilion.

[T5] Demetrios, being, as a native, well-acquainted with these places, spoke once in this way about them:
(Demetrios:) "there is indeed a hill of Mount Ida, called Kotylos and it lies about a hundred and twenty stadia above Scepsis; from it flow the Scamander, the Granicos and the Aesepus",

the two latter, towards the north and the Propontis, taking their sources from many springs, whereas the former does it from one source towards the south. All springs are, however, located close to each other in a radius of twenty stadia. The mouth of the Aesepus is located the farthest away from its source, at a distance of about five hundred stadia. There is a debate about what the poet meant by:

Il. 22. 147-52: "they reached the two fair-flowing fountains; there the two springs of the eddying Scamander have their sources. The one flows with tepid water ..."...

(which means warm). He then pursues:

..."...and all around smoke arises from it, as if from a fire. The other flows cold, as hail or wintery snow."

Indeed neither can warm water be found in the place now, nor is the spring of the Scamander there; it is instead in the mountain. Further there is one, not two of them. It is thus likely that the warm spring dried up and that the cold one, flowing from the Scamander through a subterranean channel, reappears at this place, or that, because it is close to the Scamander, also this water is called the spring of the Scamander (for, many sources are, in this way, attributed to a same river).

Strab. 13, 1, 44: And into the Scamander streams the Andiros, coming from the Karesene, a hilly region scattered with many villages and beautifully cultivated, which is adjacent to the Dardanian plain until the territories of Zeleia and Pityeia. It is said that the region takes its name from the river Karesos which is mentioned by name by the poet:

Il. 12. 20: "the Rhesos, the Heptaporos, the Karesos and the Rhodios";

but that the city which had the same name as the river was destroyed.

Translation

a) And again he (Demetrios) says as follows:

(Demetrios:) "the river Rhesus is now called Rhoeites, unless the river ** flowing into the Granikos is actually the Rhesus. "

The Heptaporos, which is also called Polyporos, is crossed seven times by those travelling from the region around the Beautiful Pine towards the village called Melaina and towards the Asclepieion, founded by Lysimachos. About this Beautiful Pine Attalos, the first king, wrote in this way:

(Attalus:) "its perimeter is 24 feet, from its root the top [rises] to a height of 67 feet; then, it is divided into three branches, each being at equally distant from the others, and then reunited again in one top, reaching all together a height of two plethra and fifteen cubits".

It is at a distance of 180 stadia from Adramyttion towards the north. The Karesos flows down from Malus, a place located between Palaescepsis and Achaiion, which belongs to the coastal territory of the Tenedians, and empties into the Aesepos. The Rhodios flows down from Kleandria and Gordos, which are at 60 stadia away from the Beautiful Pine , and empties into the Ainion.

The first place in the valley formed by the river Aisepos, located on the left of its stream, is Polichna, a place with fortifications, then comes Palaescepsis followed by Alazonion (this name has actually been fabricated in relation with the hypothesis of the Halizones, of whom I have already spoken); then comes the abandoned Karesos, the Karesene and the river with the same name, which forms also a remarkable valley, although less than the one of the Aisepos. What is beyond belongs already either to the plain of Zeleia or to its well cultivated plateau. On the right side of the Aisepos, in between Polichna and Palaescepsis, there is the village called Ainea and Argyria. This too is again a fabrication due to the same hypothesis in order to preserve the formula (Il. 2. 857) "where there is the birth of silver". Where is now Alybe, or Alope or to whatever extent they want to change the name? They should have fabricated this too, after having rubbed their forehead, and not have left it lame and subject to interrogations, because they have already dared this once.

[T4] These elements face then such objections, for the rest, however, or at least for most of it, we assume that we have to follow him as a man who is acquainted with and native of the place and who has thought so much about this, that he has written 30 books about the little more than 60 lines of the Trojan Catalogue.

He says then that Palaescepsis is 50 stadia away from Ainea and 30 from the Aisepos; that from this Palaescepsis the name was applied to many other places. But we are returning to the beach where we left it first.

b) Schol. T Il. 12. 20a: (cf. Eust. 889,59) Rhesos: it was renamed Rhoeites. It flows towards the north, down from the Beautiful Pine, which is 180 stadia away from Adramytteion.

c) Schol. T Il. 12. 20e: Rhodios: it is called Dardanos. It flows down from Kleandria, which is sixty stadia away from the Pine.

Cf. Hesych. s.v. Ῥοδίος: Rhodios: a river. The present Dardanos.

Translation

Strab. 12, 3, 20: The scholar from Scepsis also considers to change the name from “Alybes” to “Chalybes”, but, as he does not take into account what would follow from this or what would be in agreement with it, - in particular why the poet would have called the Chalybes Halizones -, he rejects this opinion (...)

Strab. 12, 3, 22: The scholar from Scepsis, accepting apparently neither this scholar's opinion nor the one from those supposing that the Halizones come from near Pallene (whom I mention in the parts on Macedonia), is likewise at lost to understand how someone could consider that an allied force could have been sent to the Trojans from among the nomads living beyond the Borysthenes. He, however, fully approves of the opinions defended by Hecataeus of Miletus, by Menecrates from Elaea, one among the disciples of Xenocrates, and by Palaephatus. Among these men, the first says in his Circuit of the Earth:

(Hecataeus:) "Then comes the city of Alazia; the river Odrysses, flowing from lake Dascylitis eastwards through the Mygdonian plain, empties into the river Rhyndacus."

He says that Alazia is now deserted, but that many villages of the Alazones, through whose territory the Odrysses flows, are inhabited; that Apollo was honoured there in a particularly intensive way, especially along the border with the Cyziceni. Menecrates, in his Circuit of the Hellespont,

(Menecrates:) "says thatabove the area belonging to Myrleia, lies a continous mountainous plateau, that is inhabited by the tribe of the Halizones. He claims that it has to be written with two lambda, but that the poet spells it with one because of the meter."

Palaephatus, finally, says

(Palaephatus:) that Odios and Epistrophos started their expedition from the Alazones who dwelt in Alope, which is now Zeleia.

Nonetheless, Demetrios pretends that those who located the Amazons near Pygela, between Ephesus, Magnesia and Priene, talk nonsense, because the formula "from far away" does not fit this place. How much more it is unfitting for the region near Mysia and Teuthrania!

Strab. 12, 3, 23: But, by Zeus, he says that it is necessary to understand that some formula have been added while being used in an improper sense, as for instance (Il. 2. 863) "far from Ascania" and (Od. 18.5) "he has the name of Arnaeus; the august mother gave it" and (Od. 21.6) "(Penelope) took the nicely bent key with her strong hand". Let’s accept this too. But one has not to accept the elements with which Demetrios, after having given them all his attention, is not able to give a convincing responds to those who maintain that one should hear the line "far away from the Chalybe". For, albeit he makes the concession that it is possible that silver-mines may have existed in the country of the Chalybes, even if they do not exist anymore today, he does, however, not agree that they were famous and worthy of notice like the iron-mines (...) What then, if the silver-mines did not reach fame in the Heroic time but in Homer’s one? Could one blame the assertion of the poet? How then did the fame come to the poet's ears? And how did the fame of the copper-mines of Temesa in Italy reach him? (Od. 1.184) (...)

[T8] But, he does not even agree with those whose opinion he is defending. For, while describing the region around Scepsis, his own hometown, he mentions near Scepsis and the Aesepos, the village Ainea, Argyria and Alazonion (...) Hecataeus(...)
